Opinion Essay — Band 8 Sample

Band 8
▼

Topic: Some people believe that university education should be free for everyone. To what extent do you agree or disagree?

It is often argued that higher education should be a fundamental right available to all citizens, regardless of their financial background. I strongly agree with this viewpoint, as I believe that educated populations bring significant social and economic benefits to society.

Firstly, when tertiary education is accessible to all, it creates a more skilled and productive workforce. For instance, countries such as Germany and Scandinavia have invested heavily in accessible higher education, resulting in highly qualified labour markets that drive innovation and economic growth. In contrast, when students are burdened with excessive student loans, many talented individuals from lower-income families are deterred from pursuing higher education altogether.

Furthermore, an educated populace contributes to stronger democratic institutions and reduced social inequality. When education is only available to the wealthy, it perpetuates cycles of poverty and limits social mobility.

In conclusion, while some contend that free university education places an unreasonable financial burden on governments, the long-term benefits to society far outweigh these concerns. Investing in accessible education is ultimately an investment in a more prosperous and equitable future.

Discussion Essay — Band 7 Sample

Band 7
▼

Topic: Some people think that children should start school earlier, while others believe they should start at age 6 or 7. Discuss both views.

The question of when children should begin formal education has long been a subject of debate among parents and educators alike. While some argue that an early start provides children with a competitive advantage, others maintain that a later start is more developmentally appropriate.

Those who advocate for early schooling argue that young children's brains are particularly receptive to learning. During the first few years of life, neural plasticity is at its peak, meaning children can absorb languages and basic numeracy concepts more easily than older children. Countries such as Finland have demonstrated that a later start does not hinder academic achievement, yet this is often balanced against the benefits of early socialisation.

On the other hand, many experts believe that children who start formal education too early may experience unnecessary stress and miss out on crucial play-based learning opportunities. Research suggests that play is not merely a leisure activity but an essential component of cognitive and social development.

In conclusion, while the cognitive benefits of early education are appealing, I believe that a play-based early childhood approach followed by formal schooling from age 6 or 7 is the most beneficial approach for the majority of children.
